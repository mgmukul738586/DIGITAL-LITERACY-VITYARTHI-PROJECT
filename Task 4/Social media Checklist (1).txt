The Do’s: Best Practices for Success
•	Curate a Professional Identity: Treat platforms like LinkedIn or portfolio sites as an extension of your resume. Share your academic achievements, projects, and certifications to demonstrate your value to potential employers.
•	Audit Your Privacy Settings Periodically: Platforms frequently update their data policies. Regularly review your visibility settings to ensure that personal content is only shared with those you trust.
•	Think Before You Post: Use the "Billboard Test"—if you wouldn't be comfortable seeing your post displayed on a giant billboard in the center of campus, do not upload it.
•	Engage with Meaningful Content: Follow industry leaders, academic organizations, and professional groups. Liking and sharing insightful articles shows you are engaged with your field of study.
•	Verify Information Before Sharing: Before hitting "repost," take a moment to fact-check the source. Stopping the spread of misinformation demonstrates critical thinking and digital literacy.

The Don’ts: Pitfalls to Avoid
•	Avoid Posting Sensitive Personal Information: Do not share your physical address, phone number, or real-time location. Wait until you have left a venue before posting photos to protect your physical security.
•	Refrain from Venting About Academic Staff or Employers: Frustrations with professors or supervisors are natural, but airing them publicly is unprofessional and can lead to disciplinary action or missed job opportunities.
•	Do Not Engage in Cyberbullying or Harassment: Avoid heated arguments or "trolling." Digital footprints are permanent; a moment of anger can be archived and linked to your name for years to come.
•	Don't Disregard Academic Integrity: Never post photos of exam papers, specific assignment prompts, or proprietary course materials. Sharing these can be flagged as a violation of university honor codes.
•	Avoid Documenting Policy Violations: Never post photos or videos that depict illegal activities or violations of campus conduct codes. Recruiters and admissions officers often screen social media as part of their background checks.
